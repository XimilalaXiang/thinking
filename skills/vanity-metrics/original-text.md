# Original Text: Key Passages

Source: [Julian Shapiro — Pick better life goals](https://www.julian.com/blog/vanity-metrics) (October 31, 2020)

The full essay is approximately 2,500 words. Below are the most important passages that form the intellectual foundation of this skill. They are ordered to follow the essay's logical flow.

---

## Opening Insight

> "If you replayed your life and took the shortest path to where you are today, you'd see what an absurd amount of time was spent on the wrong things."
>
> "Some say this is unavoidable—that you need a lot of exploring to find yourself. This isn't the full story. You can train yourself to identify when you're working on the wrong thing—in real time as it's happening."

## Definition

> "Vanity metrics are:
> 1. Intermediary goals that occur before the fulfilling goals that matter.
> 2. Easier to accomplish than our true goals.
> 3. Frequently encouraged by society."

## The Goal Path Framework

> "Vanity metrics—the metrics we're at risk of narrowly focusing on—are most commonly found near the beginning of a path. That's because the earliest steps, such as website visits and email subscribes, are the furthest from the ultimate goal."
>
> "The only time to obsess over an early step is when it's a bottleneck that prevents you from reaching the next step. If a step is not a bottleneck, you should typically 80/20 it and move on."

### On Time and Resources

> "Time and Resources are the assets that are turned into steps. As a result, they are always found at the beginning of a path. That has a huge implication: time and resources are always vanity metrics."
>
> "People boast 'I pulled an all-nighter at work' or 'I spent $100,000 on a business coach!' These are the worst vanity metrics possible."
>
> "Life is not about 'putting in the hours.' It's about seeing results and enjoying the journey."

## The Ability Spectrum Framework

> "Hard-to-game accomplishments have something in common: they create valuable output."
>
> "Whereas earning an MBA or receiving an award creates no output, creating content that people actually enjoy—as in, receives high social engagement—does."
>
> "In short, if your goal is to do more than make a quick buck, let your output be your signal—not your credentials. Smart laypeople and subject matter experts will see through credentials to focus on your output."

## The Virtue Spectrum Framework

> "How much we authentically care about something is reflected by how much meaningful change we attempt to produce."
>
> "Chronic low-effort actions can mislead you into thinking you're having a meaningful impact and that you're doing all that needs to be done. There aren't enough people left over who are actually doing the real work."
>
> "It's like pointing your finger at an injured kid in the playground so others see them—instead of helping her back to her feet."

## The Three-Question Summary

> "Am I signaling a virtue? If yes, focus on behaviors that have an impact. Otherwise, you're foregoing long-term change for short-term social validation."
>
> "Am I signaling an ability? If yes, focus on value creation that is hard to game. Otherwise, your work won't impress talented people, and it's unlikely to last."
>
> "Am I chasing an outcome? If yes, don't obsess over early steps in your goal path. Otherwise, you may never reach your ultimate goal."

## Closing Principle

> "Don't inherit goals without sanity checking them. Don't outsource them to society. By default, society prioritizes vanity metrics because even the lazy can achieve them."
